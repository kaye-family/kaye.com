Budgets Don't Work

Debit card only solves about 90% of the problem.

Nick found what worked for him was to figure out how much he could afford to spend per day, on anything and everything, after taking out his monthly expenses (rent, phone, and utilities). He only had to focus on that one number, how much have I spent today? By staying below that number every single day, any remainder built up in his bank account. Concert tickets, new laptop, vacations have to wait for that buildup, or do without.

Parental Bail-outs

Is it wise to bail our adult children out of trouble they got themselves into? Or should one let them bear the consequences, so they learn?

Unless this is a long-term chronic problem, grab your bailing bucket. Left alone, it doesn't go away, it just gets deeper.

If your own financial resources are ample enough, of course you're going to help your child onto dry land. The question is, What's the Deal?

On your side of the Deal, will you help with a gift or a loan? depends on your assessment of the circumstances.

A loan leaves the responsibility with the learner, merely eases it; major gifts are for young people who have already shown themselves to be responsible with money.

On learner's side of the deal, what conditions are you going to oppose? Write it all out, signed by both parties.

The bottom line

Trust Me discusses many ways you can help young adults fight their way back from serious debt:

         When it doesn't conflict with teaching them to meet their obligations, you can bail them out of part or all of their debt with a gift.

         You can lend them money at a significantly lower rate of interest, or no interest, and with less draconian repayment terms.

         Your Deal can specify that they won't borrow any more, from a credit card or any other source.

         You can help them slash their spending to the bone.

         If you think they need help with banking and bill paying, whether they think they do or not, your Deal can insist they take that coachingfrom someone.
