Book Review

 

IQ, Heritability and Racism. By James M. Lawler, New York: International Publishers,1979. 228 pp.

                                       

Review by Kenneth Kaye, Department of Education, University of Chicago

 

This is a readable, elementary, yet authoritative Marxist review of the controversy over the inevitability of measured differences in intellectual performance. The author exposes most of the conceptual fallacies underlying IQ tests (which have no power to discover biological traits, but merely reflect the discriminations built into them by item selection and standardization) and in the misinterpretation of heritability estimates (which have nothing to do with inevitability and nothing to do with differences between groups). He neglects a relevant, ultimately perhaps more important issue: whether intelligence is a single, general dimension along which individuals vary, or many different abilities.

All of this, of course, is old ground. What distinguishes the book from many other vigorous arguments on both sides is that it pulls no ideological punches. Professor Lawler is not the first to attack the unsupportable arguments of Jensen and Eysenck as rationalizations for continued suppression of the working class by the controlling capitalist class. He manages, however, to go beyond the popular controversy started by Jensen or even the one started by Terman 60 years ago. He correctly identifies the real controversy as the one between Marxism and social Darwinism. That conflict (more evident, recently, in the “sociobiology” debate) troubles any attempt to connect the biological sciences with the nature of man. Recasting theories in terms of left-right politics helps us recognize that ideological assumptions distort all our inferences. The problem is that revolutionary philosophers of science seem willing to assume that their own ideology, being the correct one, never distorts but merely illuminates the scientific truth. Essential to Marxism is not only the moral rightness but also the historical inevitability of socialism, and thus what began as a theory of economics and a political ideology immediately took a stand in biology. It did so also in art, in religion, in history, but it is particularly the obsession with man's transcendence over biological evolution which has kept Marxism squirming unproductively and unnecessarily for 100 years.

One need not be a Marxist to share Lawler's vision of a society without racism and of an educational system geared to creative, communicating human minds. One does need to be a Marxist to accept the argument that socialism is the inevitable product of man's cultural evolution, just as one needs social Darwinism in order to believe in the inevitable superiority of the offspring of the rich. In fact, either position is equally blinding for a scientist. Furthermore, science can no more resolve the educational issue (how should children be grouped, and how should resources be distributed?) than it can decide whether private property should be inherited or whether capital should belong to the state. It is quite right to expose the fallacies in any attempt to use heritability of IQ to justify a political position, for the IQ construct itself merely reifies the assumptions of a ruling class. But Lawler makes the same mistake so many of his predecessors in the last decade have made: he attempts to argue with the genetic determinists on their own terms. If it later turns out that Cyril Burt would have gotten similar results even if he had not fabricated his data (as, for example, Mendel would have done) or that learning ability is proved to be 99 percent heritable, Jensen et al. will appear vindicated. Yet it will still be wrong to deny any child, regardless of parentage, the fullest and best educational opportunities a human society can devise. As surely as scientific conclusions can be biased by ideology, political and moral conclusions can be obfuscated by science.

 

[reviewed in Perspectives in Biology and Medicine, Spring 1980, pp. 493-494]
